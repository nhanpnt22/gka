Reality Alignment Loop (RAL-H)
=============================
Author: Phạm Ngọc Thanh Nhân (Brian Pham)
Final Version: March 26, 2026

Definition
----------
Reality Alignment Loop (RAL-H) is a minimal, self-correcting decision system that aligns beliefs, decisions, and problem framing with reality through a closed loop of probabilistic prediction, action, observation, and error-driven adaptation, augmented with constraints that ensure survival, robustness, and reliability under uncertainty.

A system qualifies as RAL-H only if, over sufficient time horizons:
- Predictive accuracy improves
- Real-world outcomes measurably improve
- Adaptation measurably responds to new information and environmental change

--------------------------------------------------

Core Mechanism (Irreducible Loop)
--------------------------------

Predict → Act → Observe → Update

Predict:
- State expected outcomes with explicit probabilities
- Maintain irreducible uncertainty (no probability = 0 or 1)

Act:
- Choose action via:
  - Protect (limit downside)
  - Explore (reduce uncertainty)
  - Exploit (maximize expected value)
- Enforce:
  → No action with irreversible downside
  → Action size proportional to confidence and evidence quality

Observe:
- Record actual real-world outcomes
- Use proxy metrics only if explicitly defined and validated against real outcomes
- Assign confidence weights to observations based on source reliability and potential manipulation

Update:
- Compare prediction vs outcome to compute error
- Reweight models by predictive accuracy over time (calibration and outcome alignment)
- Adjust models and problem framing based on error

If any step is skipped:
→ learning fails
→ misalignment compounds over time

--------------------------------------------------

Core Properties
---------------

1. Reality as Final Authority
- Truth is determined only by observable real-world outcomes
- Beliefs without predictive performance have zero authority

2. Error-Driven Learning
- Learning occurs only through prediction–outcome mismatch
- Persistent error forces revision or removal of models

3. Probabilistic Structure
- All predictions are probabilistic
- Uncertainty is explicit, bounded, and continuously updated

4. Self-Correcting Dynamics
- Models are continuously reweighted by predictive accuracy over time
- Inaccurate models lose influence; accurate models gain influence

5. Adaptive Framing
- Objectives, variables, constraints, and assumptions are continuously tested
- Persistent mismatch triggers reframing of the problem

6. Adaptive Action Selection
- High downside → Protect
- High uncertainty or rising error → Explore
- Stable accuracy → Exploit

7. Model Competition
- Multiple models compete implicitly
- Survival is determined by predictive performance over time

--------------------------------------------------

Hardening Layers (Resilience Constraints)
----------------------------------------

1. Uncertainty Floor
- All probabilities are bounded away from 0 and 1

2. Downside Constraint
- Irreversible downside is defined as loss from which recovery is not feasible within the system’s operating time horizon
- Actions violating this constraint are invalid

3. Dual-Track Validation
- Proxy metrics must remain aligned with real-world outcomes
- Divergence invalidates the proxy

4. Adversarial Awareness
- Observations are weighted by reliability and potential manipulation
- Low-confidence data reduces model update strength

5. Regime Shift Detection
- Sustained increase in error triggers:
  → reduced model confidence
  → increased exploration
  → reassessment of assumptions

6. Time Horizon Calibration
- Each decision defines:
  → evaluation window
  → expected feedback delay

7. Meta-Learning Loop
- The system evaluates:
  → prediction calibration
  → metric validity
  → feedback reliability
  → adaptation speed and direction

8. Action Size Control
- Exposure scales with:
  → confidence
  → evidence quality
  → downside risk

9. Exploration Floor
- A minimum level of exploration is always maintained to prevent stagnation

--------------------------------------------------

System Behavior
---------------

RAL-H makes error unstable and accuracy self-reinforcing:

- Inaccurate models → poor predictions → error → reduced influence → correction
- Accurate models → better predictions → increased influence → reinforcement

Sustained misalignment becomes structurally unstable and is forced to correct or collapse.

--------------------------------------------------

Validity Conditions (Strict)
---------------------------

RAL-H is valid only if, over time:

1. Predictive accuracy improves (calibration increases)
2. Real-world outcomes improve (not just proxies)
3. Adaptation measurably responds to new information and environmental change

If not:
- The system is misapplied
- Or mismeasured
- Or operating under insufficient or low-quality feedback

--------------------------------------------------

Explicit Limits
---------------

RAL-H does not guarantee:
- Immediate correctness
- Immunity to noisy, delayed, or adversarial feedback
- Stability under extreme uncertainty or rare events

Performance must be evaluated over sufficient time horizons to distinguish signal from noise.

It guarantees only:
- Progressive alignment with reality given sufficient feedback quality, iteration, and time
- Survival under bounded risk constraints

--------------------------------------------------

One-Line Essence
---------------

RAL-H is a closed-loop system that uses probabilistic prediction, error feedback, and survival constraints to continuously enforce alignment between thinking and reality under real-world uncertainty.
